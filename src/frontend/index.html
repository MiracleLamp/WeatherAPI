How do thunderstorms grow?

How does a thunderstorm form? Three basic ingredients are required for a thunderstorm to form: moisture, rising unstable air (air that keeps rising when given a nudge), and a lifting mechanism to provide the “nudge.” The sun heats the surface of the earth, which warms the air above it.

Thunderstorm clouds types explanation

Each type of thunderstorm has its own special characteristics in terms of size, amount of precipitation, wind speed, hail size and thunderstorm intensity. Single cell storm differ in their relatively small size and precipitation area, however, it can cause such a phenomenon as microburst, this phenomenon in which a huge mass of precipitation falls at once, which causes destructive winds. However, such thunderstorms rarely turn out to be dangerous and usually fall apart within 30 minutes. Multicells - the most common type of cells is a cluster of single cells at different stages of development, in multicells it is possible to form an extensive wind coming from the side of the cloud, the electrical activity of multicells can be quite high. A supercell is a monocell in which rotation has begun, this happens due to strong wind shifts in the atmosphere. This is the rarest type of thunderstorms, but also the most dangerous, this type of thunderstorms is able to generate very powerful precipitation in a short time and cause floods, as well as destructive winds associated with powerful microbursts, 0 visibility, giant hail and the main cherry on the cake is a tornado. This type of thunderstorms most often occurs in the United States, but they also occur everywhere in other parts of the world, however, a little less often. Huge hail can fall in supercells up to 5-10 cm, and sometimes even higher.
